Ongoing Projects

  • Resolving the hubble tension using Pulsar Timing

    May 29, 2025

    This project explores the possibility of measuring the Hubble constant using pulsar timing data, independent of traditional cosmological probes. By modeling the effect of cosmic expansion on pulsar time-of-arrival (TOA) measurements, I simulate the Hubble-induced delay and extract its signature from residuals using quadratic fitting. This approach offers an alternative pathway to address the Hubble tension and complements existing methods such as using Type 1A supernovae as standard candles.

  • Solar Wind Gaussian Process (SWGP) Modeling

    March 6, 2025

    I am currently working on the Gaussian Process modelling solar wind effects in pulsar timing arrays (PTAs). This involves analyzing the influence of solar wind plasma on pulsar timing data, separating its effects from other noise sources like dispersion measure variations and red noise. My research focuses on understanding correlations between solar wind parameters, electron column density, and their impact on pulsar timing precision.

Publications

  • Fewer supermassive binary black holes in pulsar timing array observations

    September, 2024

    Abstract: We find the inferred properties of the putative gravitational wave background in the second data release of the European Pulsar Timing Array (EPTA) to be in better agreement with theoretical expectations under the improved noise model. In particular, if the gravitational wave background is from supermassive black hole binaries, the observed gravitational wave emission is the dominant source of the binary energy loss, with no evidence of environmental effects, eccentric orbits, or an overabundance of sources. Our improved noise model addresses the misspecification of noise priors which impacts the inference of temporal correlations of the background. We also find evidence for epoch-correlated temporally-uncorrelated (white) noise which has a stronger impact on the measurement in the presence of Hellings-Downs correlations of the background.

    Here is the Link to the paper: Fewer supermassive binary black holes in pulsar timing array observations

  • Ensemble noise properties of the European Pulsar Timing Array

    September, 2024

    Abstract: The null hypothesis in Pulsar Timing Array (PTA) analyses includes assumptions about ensemble properties of pulsar time-correlated noise. These properties are encoded in prior probabilities for the amplitude and the spectral index of the power-law power spectral density of temporal correlations of the noise. In this work, we introduce a new procedure for numerical marginalisation over the uncertainties in pulsar noise priors. The procedure may be used in searches for nanohertz gravitational waves and other PTA analyses to resolve prior misspecification at negligible computational cost. Furthermore, we infer the distribution of amplitudes and spectral indices of the power spectral density of spin noise and dispersion measure variation noise based on the observation of 25 millisecond pulsars by the European Pulsar Timing Array (EPTA). Our results may be used for the simulation of realistic noise in PTAs.

Research works I found interesting

  • Cosmic Clocks Could Help Solve the Universe's Biggest Speeding Discrepancy!

    May 29, 2025

    Imagine trying to measure how fast a city is growing. One team uses old maps (like clues from the early universe), while another surveys its current expansion. What if their answers don't match? That's the "Hubble Tension" in cosmology – a major disagreement on how fast our universe is expanding today (the Hubble constant, H₀).

    So, how can we get an independent check? My research explores using pulsars: incredibly dense, spinning remnants of dead stars. They flash beams of light like cosmic lighthouses, and we detect their pulses with amazing regularity – they're nature's most precise clocks.

    Here's the core idea: as the universe expands, the light from a distant pulsar stretches, slightly delaying each pulse's arrival. Crucially, the universe's expansion rate isn't constant; it's accelerating! My work shows this changing expansion should cause a tiny, cumulative, time-dependent shift in the pulsar signals over many years. This would appear as a subtle drift in when we expect the pulses versus when they actually arrive.

    The "slope" of this drift is directly tied to the Hubble constant. If we can measure this incredibly small effect, we’ll have a completely new way to determine H₀. It’s like adding an expert surveyor who listens to the universe's own ticking, potentially helping to finally resolve the Hubble Tension.

    This graph shows the key idea: as the universe expands, light from distant pulsars (cosmic lighthouses) gets stretched. Over many years, this causes a tiny, cumulative delay in their pulse arrival times, seen here as a clear downward slope (the black line). The steepness of this slope tells us how fast the universe is expanding (the Hubble constant, H₀). In this test, the method accurately recovered the known expansion rate, showing its potential as a new cosmic ruler.

  • Is Dark Energy a Cosmic Illusion? Supernovae Hint at a New Universe Model

    Mar 7, 2025

    For decades, scientists have believed a mysterious "dark energy" is pushing our universe to expand ever faster. This is a cornerstone of the standard ΛCDM (Lambda Cold Dark Matter) model. But what if there's another explanation?

    New research, analyzing data from distant exploding stars (Type Ia supernovae – our "cosmic lighthouses"), suggests an alternative model called "timescape cosmology" fits the observations better. Instead of dark energy, timescape proposes that the universe's clumpy structure – vast empty voids and dense galaxy filaments – influences its overall expansion through what's called "backreaction." Essentially, the energy from expanding voids could be what we've been interpreting as dark energy.

    Using an improved statistical method on the comprehensive Pantheon+ supernovae dataset, scientists found "very strong evidence" favoring the timescape model over the standard dark energy model. Even when looking only at very distant supernovae, where the universe should be smoother, timescape still held an edge.

    This doesn't mean dark energy is dead, but it’s a significant nudge. These findings suggest we might need to fundamentally rethink how the universe works on the grandest scales, potentially opening a new chapter in cosmology.
